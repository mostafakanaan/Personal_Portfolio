# From Syria to Switzerland: A Developer's Journey

I grew up in Aleppo. I write code in Zurich. The distance between those two sentences is about a decade of my life.

## Starting Over

When you move to a new country, everything resets. Your credentials, your network, your language. In Switzerland, even if you're fluent in English and German, you quickly learn that Swiss German (Züritüütsch) is a third language entirely — and it's not written anywhere, so you can't even study it from a book.

I dealt with this the same way I deal with new codebases: **read everything, ask questions without shame, build small things first**.

## Learning German (And Swiss German)

My approach to language learning is embarrassingly programmer-brained:

1. **Anki** for vocabulary (spaced repetition = gradient descent for memory)
2. **Immersion** — only German-language YouTube, podcasts, TV
3. **Output pressure** — I forced myself to take a German-language job before I felt "ready"

The last point was the most important. You don't get fluent by studying. You get fluent by being in situations where fluency is required for survival.

```
Fluency = f(uncomfortable_situations, time)
Studying ≈ 0 * time = 0
```

## The Swiss Job Market

Switzerland is interesting for developers. The market is strong, the salaries are high, but the expectations are also high — and the culture is *very* different from what I was used to.

Things that surprised me:

- **Precision is valued above speed** — Swiss culture genuinely prefers a slower, more careful approach over "move fast and break things"
- **Punctuality is non-negotiable** — being 3 minutes late to a meeting is a genuine cultural offense
- **Directness is not rudeness** — Swiss people say exactly what they mean, which I actually prefer

## What I'd Tell My Past Self

1. Your accent is not a handicap. In tech, results speak louder than pronunciation.
2. Learn the local language *before* you think you need it.
3. The imposter syndrome never fully goes away — you just get better at working with it.
4. Build things in public. GitHub is the universal portfolio.

---

I'm still learning, still building, still occasionally confused by Swiss German. But I'm here, and I'm doing the work. That's enough.
